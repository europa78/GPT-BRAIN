## Proposal Formats and When to Use Them  

### Infographic Proposals  
Infographics distill information into a **visually engaging**, easy-to-digest format. In B2B marketing, they are powerful for **top-of-funnel outreach and lead generation**. Additionally, humans process visuals **60,000 times faster** than text, and around **65% of people are visual learners**【19†L107-L115】. A well-designed infographic ensures your core message or value proposition is understood quickly – a big advantage when a busy property manager or contractor is skimming through emails or social feeds. 

**When and How to Use:** Use infographics primarily in the **awareness stage** of marketing and as a **supporting element** in proposals. They work well as standalone pieces in blog posts, marketing emails, or LinkedIn updates to spark initial interest (for example, sharing an infographic of home energy savings achieved through efficient HVAC systems). They typically should **not** be gated behind a form – instead, infographics attract eyeballs and then guide viewers toward deeper content【16†L75-L83】. In a proposal document or slide deck, an infographic can break up text and illustrate a point (e.g. a timeline of your project process, or a before-and-after comparison of results). The key is relevance and clarity: ensure any infographic included actually reinforces your message and isn’t just decorative. “The best infographics make data easy to understand…getting to the heart of the matter succinctly and with flair,” notes Michael Keegan of Pew Research【19†L113-L121】. ### PDF Documents (Detailed Proposals & White Papers)  
PDF is the classic format for formal business proposals and longer content like white papers, case studies, or service brochures. In home services B2B marketing, a PDF proposal is often the **go-to format for mid-to-late stage communications** – for example, a detailed **bid or service proposal** sent to a building manager, or a downloadable **white paper** on “Modern Landscaping ROI for Commercial Properties” offered on your website to capture leads. PDFs are popular because of their **stability and universal compatibility**: they preserve formatting across devices and can be opened almost anywhere without special software【10†L39-L48】. This is crucial when sending a proposal to multiple stakeholders – you can trust that your carefully formatted content (charts, images, branding) will appear as intended. PDFs also have relatively **small file sizes** and can be easily emailed or shared via cloud links【10†L53-L57】, which is convenient when prospects expect an attachment or printable document. Another advantage is that PDFs feel “official” – a nicely designed PDF with your company logo, a cover page, table of contents, etc., gives the impression of a comprehensive, professional offer (almost like a mini-book or report) that decision-makers can review at their own pace. 

**When and How to Use:** Use PDF proposals in the **consideration and decision stages** of B2B sales. For instance, after an initial call or meeting, you might follow up by emailing a PDF that outlines the specific services and pricing you can offer to that client. Or, if responding to an RFP, you’ll likely compile the response as a PDF document following the required structure. Home services companies also often prepare **standard PDF proposal templates** that sales reps can customize per client – these include sections like an executive summary, company info, scope of work, timeline, proof of insurance/licenses, client testimonials, and a contract or sign-off page. When creating PDF proposals, ensure they are **visually appealing and skimmable**. Break up long text with headings, bullet points, imagery and **data visualizations (tables, charts, icons)**. While PDFs are static by nature, you can still make them engaging: include your branding, perhaps a touch of color from your logo, and even embed hyperlinks to your website or portfolio. One limitation is that PDFs lack interactivity – no animations or embedded video – and can feel dry【10†L61-L69】【10†L73-L77】. To counter this, treat the design like a **slide deck on paper**: use graphics or even mini-infographics where possible, and keep paragraphs concise. Also consider the reading experience on different devices; while PDFs are universally viewable, reading a multi-page PDF on a phone can be tedious. Many B2B buyers **use mobile devices throughout their buying journey (80% do)**, so ensure your PDF text is large enough and layout is simple for small screens【39†L653-L661】 (e.g. single-column layouts, no tiny fonts). Another best practice is to include a **table of contents with clickable links** for easy navigation, especially if your proposal runs more than a few pages【12†L169-L175】. And remember to incorporate **client-specific details** – a personalized cover letter or introduction in the PDF can set the tone and show the prospect you understand their unique needs (e.g. mention their facility or goals explicitly, not just a generic “dear client” message). Overall, PDFs are best used when a prospect is seriously evaluating your offering and needs **comprehensive information** in a format they can save, print, or forward to others. They deliver depth and professionalism, but should be enhanced with strong content (analysis, case studies, visuals) so they don’t become just “very long emails” that get skimmed and forgotten.

Slide decks also shine in **face-to-face or virtual presentations**: you can speak to the slides, use builds/animations to reveal points stepwise, and engage in conversation, using the deck as a framework. In fact, PowerPoint was designed for exactly this style of communication and supports presenter notes, so you can have cues while delivering the proposal【10†L93-L101】【11†L188-L196】.

### Email Copy as a Proposal Medium  
Email remains a **critical platform** for B2B communication – it’s often the first touchpoint and the channel through which proposals are delivered or discussed. In the context of B2B proposals, “email copy” refers to the content of the emails you send to prospects, which can itself serve as a **mini-proposal** or at least an introduction to one. For example, a cold outreach email to a property manager might *propose* a meeting by briefly outlining how your HVAC service could save them 20% in energy costs, essentially functioning as a short-form proposal. More commonly, email is how you **submit a formal proposal or follow up**: you may attach a PDF or include a link to a slide deck, but the email body needs to persuade the recipient to actually open and read it. This is why experts emphasize that the proposal submission email is “the real first impression” – often even more than a cover letter inside a PDF【23†L183-L191】. If the email is poorly written or unclear, the prospect may never engage with the attached/full proposal. On the flip side, a well-crafted email can warm the reader up and guide them into the proposal document. 

**When and How to Use:** Use strong email copy at **every stage of the proposal process**: initial outreach, scheduling discovery calls, delivering the proposal, and post-proposal follow-ups. In early lead generation, your email itself might contain key selling points (akin to an elevator pitch) – essentially a text-based proposal. Keep those short and focused on the prospect’s pain point and your value proposition (e.g. “We helped a building like yours cut plumbing maintenance costs by 30%. Can we discuss doing the same for you?”). For proposal delivery, your email should **encourage the client to view the attached or linked proposal** by conveying enthusiasm, recapping the opportunity, and highlighting one or two enticing points. One recommended approach is to include a **mini executive summary** in the email body – 2–3 sentences summarizing the key benefits or solution, in case the recipient doesn’t immediately open the full proposal. Alternatively, some prefer a very **short and sweet email** that simply says “Thank you for your time, our proposal is attached, looking forward to next steps” to let the proposal speak for itself【23†L231-L239】【24†L1-L4】. The right approach depends on your industry culture and the client’s personality; either way, *clarity and professionalism are paramount*. Best practices for proposal emails include: use a descriptive yet concise **subject line** (e.g. “ABC Landscaping – Proposal for [Client Name]”), keep the tone **on-brand and courteous**, double-check grammar and formatting (clients will judge your attention to detail from the email【23†L193-L202】), and include a **call-to-action** – typically suggesting a next meeting or inviting questions【24†L1-L4】. An example email might be: 

> *Hi [Client],*  
> *Thank you for the opportunity to discuss your cleaning needs. As promised, I’ve prepared a proposal detailing how **[Your Company]** can maintain your facilities to the highest standard while reducing your current costs. You can view it here: [link].*  
> *I’m confident you’ll find our plan aligns well with your objectives (especially the section on green cleaning improving indoor air quality). Let’s schedule a short call next week to address any questions and discuss the next steps toward a partnership.*  
> *Thank you,*  
> *[You]* 

This approach politely builds on prior conversation, gives a **teaser** of value (improved air quality), points to the full proposal, and explicitly asks for a meeting – all within a brief message. Remember that many decision-makers read emails on their phones; long blocks of text will lose them. Keep emails tight and purposeful. It’s also wise to use email for **follow-ups** if you haven’t heard back – a short reminder or a piece of new info (“I thought you might be interested in a recent case study we did for a client in your industry…”) can re-engage a prospect without being pushy. Given that **41% of B2B buyers say email is influential in their purchasing decisions**【35†L27-L31】 and cold email response rates, while small, can significantly improve with personalization and the right value pitch, mastering proposal emails is vital. Think of your email copy and your formal proposals as working together: the email is the **hook and facilitator** that ensures your more detailed proposal content actually gets considered by the client.

### Other Formats and Emerging Platforms  
Beyond the core formats above, several other types of proposal delivery have gained traction, especially in digital-first marketing. These include **interactive web-based proposals**, videos, and collaborative documents. Such formats can complement or even replace traditional proposals in some cases, offering unique advantages for engagement and analytics.

- **Interactive Web Proposals:** These are proposals built as web pages or in specialized proposal software (e.g. Qwilr, Proposify, Better Proposals) that the client can access via a link. They often **combine elements of slides and PDFs** – the client can scroll or click through sections, and you can incorporate interactive elements like hovering tooltips, embedded forms (for the client to, say, select service options or provide feedback), videos, and even live data. The big advantage here is engagement and insight: interactive proposals not only look impressive, but they allow you to **track the client’s interaction** (e.g. you can see which sections they spent the most time on, whether they forwarded it, etc., via the proposal software). Sales professionals report that such interactive proposals and digital brochures can generate **twice as many conversions** compared to static PDFs【33†L113-L120】. They also differentiate your company – **88% of brands** say that interactive content helps distinguish them from competitors【33†L113-L120】. For home services, you might use a web proposal to present a slick, multimedia experience: for instance, a roofing contractor could show before/after drone footage of roof inspections within the proposal, or a lawn care company could embed a short personalized video message from the CEO welcoming the prospective client. When sending an interactive proposal, you typically email a link (so the email tips above still apply). Make sure to highlight any **call-to-action** within the web proposal, like a “Accept and Sign” button or scheduling link, to streamline closing. One consideration: always have a PDF export or summary available, as some more traditional clients may insist on a file they can download for their records.

- **Video Proposals/Presentations:** Video can be used in multiple ways. One is embedding video into the above-mentioned web proposals or even into a PowerPoint (though file size becomes an issue for the latter). Another is recording a **video walkthrough** of your proposal. For example, using a screen recording tool, you can create a 5-minute video of you flipping through the proposal slides or document and explaining key points. This can be sent to busy stakeholders who appreciate a “guided tour” at their convenience. Personalized video messages (even a simple 1-minute webcam introduction appended to an email) can build trust and put a face to your company, which is valuable in partnership development. In home services, trust is key – facility managers need to know the people behind the service are reliable. A warm, professional video in which you say, “Hi [Client], we put together a proposal to improve your building’s energy efficiency – let me highlight the main points for you…” can make your approach more memorable. According to marketing surveys, video is considered the **second most effective B2B marketing content type after in-person events**【39†L723-L731】. So when in-person meetings are not possible, video is a strong proxy. Use it to complement written proposals, especially if you suspect a proposal PDF alone might not get the attention it deserves. Tools like Vidyard or Loom even let you see if the prospect watched the video, adding to your lead intelligence.

- **Collaborative Documents:** In some partnership scenarios, you might co-author a proposal with the client (for instance, drafting a **joint partnership plan**). Platforms like Google Docs or Microsoft Word online allow shared editing. While not a traditional “proposal format,” this approach can work for less formal proposals such as a **memorandum of understanding or a joint marketing plan** between two companies. The benefit is both parties can input and there’s a sense of co-ownership. If using this, ensure to follow up with a more formal document or email confirming the final terms.

- **Social Media and Online Platforms:** Don’t overlook the power of social platforms for distributing “proposal-like” content to generate leads. As highlighted earlier, **LinkedIn is a major channel** – 80% of B2B marketers use it to promote content, and 4 out of 5 B2B social media leads come from LinkedIn【39†L735-L743】. Home services firms can post short proposal previews or insights (for example, a carousel post which is essentially a mini slide deck or infographic) to attract potential clients. These aren’t proposals directed at one client, but they mimic proposal content (showcasing your results, your process, your value) in a public way to stimulate inbound inquiries.

To choose among these various formats, consider the **level of personalization needed, the client’s tech-savviness, and the stage of the deal**. A general rule of thumb: earlier-stage and broad outreach = **more visual and shareable** (infographics, social posts, short videos); later-stage and specific opportunity = **more detailed and interactive** (tailored slide deck, full PDF, interactive link, or live presentation). Often, a **combination** works best – for example, start with an infographic or blog post to reel in a lead, nurture with a white paper download (PDF) that establishes your expertise, then when a prospect shows serious interest, deliver a customized slide deck in a meeting, and follow up with an interactive proposal link and a friendly email recap. By meeting prospects across multiple formats, you accommodate their preferences and increase the likelihood of engagement. Remember, different stakeholders may consume content differently: a technical facility engineer might appreciate a data-heavy PDF appendix, while a CFO just wants an executive summary slide or a 1-minute video highlight of ROI.
