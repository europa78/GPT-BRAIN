Even if things seem scary, pros say it's important to look at the big picture - not the day-to-day returns
Maybe you're just a few months away from retirement, or your kid is about to start college in September - and now, you're suddenly looking at a stock market that has declined about 6% in the past week. Is it time to panic?
MarketWatch spoke with several financial advisers and experts about how to react to market losses like those we've seen in recent trading days (and especially on Monday). Their advice? It's generally wise to stay the course and not overreact, but there are nevertheless lessons that can be learned and steps that can be taken in response to such volatility.
Here are five takeaways.
So, should anyone be panicking?
As long as you've been investing in a sound way, with a portfolio that's designed to meet your short- and long-term financial goals, the decline shouldn't be an issue, the pros say. The point is that, for most people, investing is about meeting those goals over a period of time, not making a quick killing in the market.
So instead of looking at how much your investments have declined, you really should be looking at whether or not that decline has a significant impact on your financial future. In other words, it's not about the S&P 500 SPX itself, but about your personal rate of return and whether it's where it needs to be.
"At the end of the day, that's all that matters," said  Todd Stankiewicz , president of  Sykon Capital , a wealth- management firm in  Harrison, N.Y. 
Let's not forget to put things into context
As bad as the recent losses may feel to investors, the pros say you need to take into consideration the fact that the market is still up this year. In fact, the S&P 500 has increased more than 8% since the start of 2024, which translates into the kind of annual gain that most investors are glad to see.
In effect, the losses accrued through Monday set the market back to where it was a few months ago. "If you weren't unhappy in May, you shouldn't be unhappy today," said  Stephen Kates , principal financial analyst for Annuity.org and RetireGuide.com.
Surely, there must be some exceptions to the panic rule?
Of course, there are exceptions. You could be a day trader who's made lots of bad bets. Or you could be overexposed to stocks in a major way within your retirement account.
"I'd say the only people who should be panicking are those who are severely misallocated and misaligned with their goals," said  Brian Matthews , head of investment strategy at Domain Money, an advisory firm based in  New York City .
Does that mean sound-minded investors shouldn't take heed?
While most investors don't need to panic, they should be paying attention to the market and gauging their reactions to it, the pros explain. While they may be on track with their goals, if a steep decline is causing them to hyperventilate, that might be a sign they need to be in a portfolio that will prompt less concern in the future.
Think of it this way: If you've been an 80/20 investor the last couple of years - meaning one who's 80% in stocks and 20% in fixed income - maybe it's time to become a 60/40 investor. In short, this is a reminder to be honest with yourself. "If you're going to take on risk, make sure it's risk you can handle," said  Dan Egan , vice president of behavioral finance and investing with investment-services firm Betterment.
But even if you can handle the risk, the market decline could also point to the need to re-examine your portfolio based on concerns you may have or opportunities you may see. For example, you might want to maintain your current percentage allocation to stocks but "adjust the components in it," noted  Nicole Strbich , managing director of financial planning for  Buckingham Advisors , an  Ohio -based firm.
How quickly to make a move with your portfolio?
Ah, that's the  $64,000  question - or maybe the  $109 trillion  question, since that's how much is roughly invested in global stock markets (give or take a few trillion, depending on the day's results).
Even under the direst of circumstances - as in, an investor with a portfolio that's indeed severely misallocated - the pros caution against sudden changes, such as dumping all or a good chunk of your stocks. "I believe it's never a good idea to make day-to-day investment allocations based on what's going on in the markets," said  Paul Karger , co- founder of TwinFocus, a  Boston -based wealth-advisory firm.
While there's no set rule, most advisors caution you to wait at least a few days and perhaps longer to see how the situation in the market plays out. The idea is that you're not day trading but, again, pursuing a goal within some kind of extended time frame. And if you do decide to make portfolio changes, it's probably best to make them over time - meaning incremental tweaks, as opposed to a big allocation adjustment. "You should phase it in," said Karger.
