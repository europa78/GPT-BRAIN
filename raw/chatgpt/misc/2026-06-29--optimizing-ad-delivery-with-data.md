Introduction to reach and frequency buying

Advertisers often use reach and frequency as metrics as they plan a campaign. Reach refers to the number of people exposed to your ad and frequency refers to the number of times an individual sees your ad. 



Reach and frequency buying enables you to reach an established number of people within a target audience, at a specified frequency and at a fixed price. 



You can use reach and frequency buying when you want to maximize your brand exposure across an audience of 200,000 or more people. 

Advertisers often use this buying type with the following campaign objectives:

Reach

Brand awareness

Video views

How does reach and frequency buying work
Reach and frequency buying enables you to book campaigns in advance with predictable, optimized reach and controlled frequency. When you create a campaign with reach and frequency, you agree to pay a set price for the advertising inventory placement you reserve. The cost of your reach and frequency campaigns is always charged in cost per thousand impressions (CPM).



For certain advertising objectives, such as reach, brand awareness and video views, it's often more efficient to reach more people than target ads to a fewer people with a more precise audience.

Reach and frequency terminology
These are some common terms used in reach and frequency buying:

Reach

The number of people who saw your ads at least once during a campaign. Reach differs from impressions, which can include multiple views from the same people.

Frequency cap

The maximum number of times an individual sees your ad in a certain time frame.

Retargeting

Delivering ads to an audience based on their previous interaction with your ads.

Frequency

The average number of times each person saw your ad.

Sequencing
Delivering ads in a specific order so that you can tell a story over a series of ads within a campaign.

CPM

The average cost of 1,000 impressions.

Our ad delivery system works to find the best results based on advertiser goals, but it still observes the constraints that an advertiser sets, such as audience, placement, budget, bid and optimization.  

Advertisers should be thoughtful about how they use controls. Too many constraints can make it difficult for the delivery system to find enough conversion events. This can then limit how Meta technologies algorithms learn and optimize your campaigns. More constraints also means that your ads don't run as frequently, which makes it harder for your ad sets to exit the learning phase.

For example, when you narrow your target audience or set a low bid cap, you limit the options the ad delivery system can explore. As a result, you restrict its ability to find low-cost results for you.  

When possible, set fewer constraints and use them only when there’s business justification to do so. Constraints make the delivery system less flexible, reduce liquidity and diminish the ability of the system to work on your behalf and optimize with its machine learning algorithms.

We recommend you use campaign budget optimization and automatic placements during the initial setup of your campaigns, because this adds more liquidity into the ad delivery system. In other words, it gives Meta technologies more flexibility and a wider range of possible variables to choose from, which in turn helps us to better optimize for you.

Ideally, marketers would be able to give machine learning-enabled ad delivery systems free rein to achieve maximum liquidity, which is the condition in which every dollar can flow to the most valuable impression.



Strict advertiser controls reduce liquidity and can result in under-delivery or high costs for your campaigns.



Common restrictions to liquidity



Targeting: The audiences audiences are too small.
Placement: Ad sets are too small or too few to generate enough ad impressions.
Budget: The budget is too small to allow Meta technologies to reach the 50 conversions per week.
Bid: The bid is too low or the bid cap is too low.
Optimization: There aren't enough conversion events (optimization is too low in the funnel).
