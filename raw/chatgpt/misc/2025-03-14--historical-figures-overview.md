### Major Achievements/Activities:

- **Rise to Power:** Stalin became a close associate of Vladimir Lenin during the Bolshevik Revolution in 1917. Following Lenin’s death in 1924, he strategically maneuvered to eliminate political rivals, securing his position as the absolute leader of the Soviet Union by the late 1920s.

- **Industrialization and Collectivization:** Stalin implemented ambitious policies of rapid industrialization through Five-Year Plans, transforming the Soviet Union from a primarily agrarian society into a leading industrial power. His forced collectivization of agriculture, however, led to massive famines, notably the devastating Holodomor famine (1932–1933), particularly impacting Ukraine and resulting in millions of deaths.

- **Great Purges (1936–1938):** Stalin initiated mass campaigns of political repression, known as the Great Purges or Great Terror. Millions were imprisoned in the Gulag labor camps, and hundreds of thousands were executed, targeting political rivals, military leaders, intellectuals, and ordinary citizens accused of disloyalty.

- **World War II Leadership (1941–1945):** Stalin led the USSR through World War II, initially shaken by the surprise German invasion ("Operation Barbarossa") in 1941, but subsequently becoming central to the Allied victory. The Soviet Union’s victory at Stalingrad (1943) and the capture of Berlin (1945) significantly shaped post-war Europe.

- **Cold War Era:** Stalin's aggressive post-war policies, including the establishment of communist governments in Eastern Europe, triggered a prolonged geopolitical tension between the USSR and Western powers, laying the foundation for the Cold War.

---

### Historical Impact:

- Stalin profoundly reshaped global politics and the Soviet Union’s economy and society. While credited for modernizing the Soviet Union's industrial infrastructure and playing a critical role in defeating Nazi Germany, his brutal regime and political oppression left a legacy of widespread suffering, human rights abuses, and millions of deaths.

- Stalin’s policies and leadership style deeply influenced communist and authoritarian regimes worldwide and permanently impacted Russia’s political and social structures.

- His leadership significantly contributed to the Cold War dynamics that defined international politics for the second half of the 20th century.

### Cause of Death:

Joseph Stalin died on March 5, 1953, officially recorded as a cerebral hemorrhage (stroke). On March 1, he was discovered partially paralyzed at his dacha (country residence) outside Moscow. He suffered severe neurological impairment and remained semi-conscious until his death a few days later. Persistent historical debate surrounds the exact circumstances, with theories ranging from natural causes exacerbated by his declining health (hypertension, heavy smoking, and arteriosclerosis) to speculation about possible poisoning. However, no conclusive historical evidence supports assassination theories, and the widely accepted cause remains natural causes related to stroke complications.

### Major Achievements/Activities:

- **World War II Espionage:**  
  Allen Dulles played a significant role as the head of the Office of Strategic Services (OSS) station in Bern, Switzerland, during WWII, facilitating espionage operations against Nazi Germany. His contacts and intelligence gathering were instrumental in the eventual Allied victory.

- **Director of Central Intelligence (CIA):**  
  Appointed by President Dwight D. Eisenhower, Dulles was the first civilian director of the Central Intelligence Agency (1953–1961). Under his leadership, the CIA grew in influence and scope, conducting clandestine operations to counter the spread of communism during the Cold War.

- **Notable CIA Operations:**
  - **Operation Ajax (1953):** Engineered the overthrow of Iran's democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh to reinstate the Shah, altering Middle Eastern geopolitics profoundly.
  - **Guatemala Coup (1954):** Authorized the CIA operation that ousted Guatemalan President Jacobo Árbenz, perceived as sympathetic to communist causes, significantly influencing U.S. interventionism in Latin America.
  - **U-2 Spy Plane Incident (1960):** Oversaw intelligence operations involving aerial reconnaissance of the Soviet Union. The shoot-down of pilot Francis Gary Powers led to a major diplomatic crisis between the U.S. and USSR.

- **Bay of Pigs Invasion (1961):**  
  Dulles was deeply involved in planning the failed CIA-backed invasion of Cuba aimed at overthrowing Fidel Castro. The failure contributed directly to his resignation as CIA director later that year.

- **Warren Commission (1963–1964):**  
  After his CIA tenure, President Lyndon B. Johnson appointed Dulles as a member of the Warren Commission, responsible for investigating President John F. Kennedy's assassination. His role in the commission sparked controversy due to previous tensions between Kennedy and the CIA during Dulles's leadership.

---

### Historical Impact:

- Dulles significantly shaped American foreign policy and the evolution of the CIA during the Cold War era. His advocacy of covert operations and regime-change tactics became emblematic of U.S. Cold War strategy, fundamentally influencing global geopolitics, especially in Latin America and the Middle East.

- His aggressive approach toward communist containment had lasting effects on U.S. foreign policy, contributing to international tensions, geopolitical instability, and long-lasting distrust towards U.S. interventions abroad.

- The long-term outcomes of his covert actions in Iran and Latin America continue to resonate in modern geopolitics, fueling regional distrust of American foreign policy.

---

### Death and Cause:

Allen Dulles died on January 29, 1969, due to complications of influenza, which developed into pneumonia. He was 75 years old. At the time of his death, Dulles had already retired from active political life, though he remained influential through his published works and extensive commentary on intelligence affairs until his passing.
