## Chapter 3 — *Savage Commands* (Synopsis)

Chapter 3 explains what often fills the gap when we *can’t* genuinely ask the untimely questions: we end up living under “commands” that **push and pull us through pain/pleasure, fear/relief, status/shame**—even when we can tell they’re unreliable. fileciteturn18file1L13-L21

Callard develops two core “command systems”:

1) **The bodily command**

2) **The kinship command**

## Chapter 5 — **The Gadfly–Midwife Paradox** (Synopsis)

This chapter opens Part Two by posing a puzzle about what Socrates *is*: a gadfly who leaves people stung, embarrassed, and destabilized—or a midwife who helps them “give birth” to clearer thinking and real progress. Callard names this the **Gadfly–Midwife paradox**. fileciteturn24file2L1-L6

The tension deepens because Socrates is famous both for humility (“I know nothing”) and for repeatedly out-arguing elite thinkers in public. How can the same person be both “barren” and victorious—both cooperative helper and competitive refuter? fileciteturn24file2L7-L12
