- **Fallback:** Many local business owners don’t have LinkedIn profiles (a common data source), which means some databases might not list them【6†L31-L39】. - **Verification:** Whichever method you use, quickly sanity-check the names/titles returned. It’s important you’ve indeed got the owner or a top decision-maker – this ensures you’re targeting the right person for outreach, which improves lead quality.

## 3. Retrieve Direct Email Addresses and LinkedIn Profiles  
Once you have the owner’s name, the next step is to get their **direct email address** and **LinkedIn profile URL** for outreach. Many of Clay’s contact-finding integrations (including Apollo or Clearbit) will directly provide a verified work email and sometimes the LinkedIn URL along with the name【14†L297-L300】. If your previous step using Apollo’s “Find People” returned a contact, you likely already have their email and maybe a LinkedIn profile in the result. Add those to new columns (e.g., *Owner Email* and *Owner LinkedIn*).

- **Using Apollo or Clearbit Contacts:** If you have an Apollo integration set up, it often yields the person’s work email and LinkedIn in one go【14†L297-L300】. Similarly, Clay’s **“Find Contacts with Clearbit”** action can fetch emails for contacts at a company by role【16†L387-L395】. These sources draw from large U.S. business databases, giving you a good chance of getting a valid email for the owner.  
- **Hunter Email Finder:** If the direct integrations didn’t return an email, you can plug in an **Email Finder (e.g. Hunter.io)** using the person’s name and company domain. In Clay, you might use a pre-built **“Work Email Finder”** recipe which tries multiple providers in sequence【1†L155-L163】. This *waterfall enrichment* approach is recommended – it will attempt to find a work email via several APIs (Apollo, Hunter, Clearbit, etc.) until one returns a result【1†L155-L163】. The idea is to not rely on a single source, increasing your chances of success.  
- **Validate with Debounce:** Once an email is found (no matter the source), **verify its deliverability** by adding Clay’s **“Validate Email with Debounce”** action. This integration pings the email and returns a status (valid, invalid, risky, etc.)【12†L267-L272】. Create a column for Email Verification Status and use Debounce to ensure you only send outreach to emails that are safe and active【12†L238-L246】【12†L267-L272】. - **Fetching LinkedIn Profiles:** For each contact, it’s ideal to have their LinkedIn profile URL (useful for personalization or alternate outreach). Apollo often provides this if the person is in their database. If not, Clearbit’s person enrichment could be used (via email) to get a LinkedIn handle. Alternatively, use the AI Agent with a prompt like: *“Find the LinkedIn profile URL for [Name] who is the owner of [Company].”* This can often pinpoint the correct profile if one exists. Add the LinkedIn URL to a column. (Ensure it’s the right person by matching the company name on their profile.)  

By the end of this step, your table should have the **owner’s name, title, a verified email address, and a LinkedIn URL** for each business (where available). These are critical for reaching out to the actual decision-maker.

## 5. Add Company Size and Qualification Metrics  
To further qualify and score these leads, enrich the table with additional business metrics like **company size (employee count)** and other reputation signals. This will help prioritize outreach to the most relevant or promising prospects.

- **Company Size (Employees):** Use Clearbit’s enrichment data to pull the **employee count or size range** of each business. ## 6. Leverage Clay’s AI Agent for Fallbacks and Edge Cases  
Despite using multiple integrations, you may encounter missing data for some rows – especially given small local businesses can be “off the grid” in some respects. You can prompt: *“Search the web for the owner of [Business Name, City].”* The AI might find a news article, a business directory, or a LinkedIn/Facebook profile mentioning the owner. Many local owners have a Facebook presence even if they lack LinkedIn【6†L35-L43】. Claygent can attempt to find a Facebook profile of the owner (e.g., by searching “[Business Name] owner Facebook”) and extract the name. If it finds a name, you can cross-verify it matches the business (AI can be asked to confirm the context).  
- **Scanning the Website for Contacts:** If the AI didn’t explicitly find the owner via a quick search, another trick is to have it scan the company’s website for any personnel mentions. Prompt Claygent with something like: *“On the website [Website URL], look for the owner’s or staff names (e.g., in 'About us' or 'Team' sections).”* The agent can read through the site content to pick out names/titles. This can reveal not just the owner but also other team members, which might be useful if you need alternate contacts.  
- **Finding Any Email on Website:** When our primary methods fail to produce a direct email, use a web scraping approach as fallback. Clay has integrations (or you can use the Chrome extension manually) to scrape a website for email addresses. You might integrate a tool like **Apify or Outscraper** via Clay to extract any emails listed on the site【6†L41-L49】. Often, small businesses list a contact email (even if it’s a generic like info@ or the owner’s personal Gmail). Pull those into a column. Even if it’s not a personal email, it gives you something to use. After scraping, you can use Claygent to **match the found emails to the owner’s name** – for example, if an email is like `jane@business.com` and your owner’s name is Jane Doe, that’s likely her email. This clever use of AI to connect scraped data to the owner name was suggested to ensure you identify the correct email【6†L41-L49】.  
- **Domain-Wide Email Search:** If the website scrape yields nothing useful, another fallback is a **domain email finder** (many tools like Hunter can return a list of emails for a domain). You can use an integration (Apollo has one, and Hunter as well) to list all emails associated with the company’s domain. This might give you a few addresses (e.g., `john@, sales@, support@`). Again, use AI or your judgment to pick which could be the owner’s (look for name matches). It’s not as targeted, but it ensures no stone is left unturned.  
- **Generic Email Strategy:** In cases where *only* a generic contact email (like `info@business.com` or even a form on the site) is available, you should still capture that in your table (maybe in a “Fallback Email” column). You’ll treat these differently in outreach. For example, you might still email them but with a less personalized template, or use a subject line that gets attention to forward to the owner. In Clay, you can mark such rows with a flag (e.g., *Owner Email Found?* = No for these) so you can segment them. As one expert noted, if you can’t find the owner after all attempts, you still have a way to reach the business – just use a separate campaign for these generic contacts【6†L49-L55】. 

Using the AI agent and these fallback techniques ensures **automation covers even the hard cases**. You won’t have to manually Google each business; Clay will attempt all logical methods in sequence. The key is to order your steps: use the high-quality data sources first (Apollo, Clearbit, etc.), and let AI-driven or scraping methods fill in the gaps for the few that remain. This maximizes lead quality while minimizing manual effort【1†L155-L163】.

## 7. Structuring the Enriched Table for Outreach Success  
With all enrichment done, it’s important to structure and review your Clay table so that it’s ready for outreach. A well-organized table helps you use the data effectively, whether you’re doing personalized email campaigns or exporting to a CRM.

- **Column Layout:** Organize the new columns in a logical order next to the original data. For example:  
  - *Owner Name* and *Owner Title* – verify these are populated for all or most rows (this is your primary contact).  
  - *Owner Email* (and a separate *Email Verified* status if you used Debounce) – you want to be confident in the email before sending.  
  - *Owner LinkedIn URL* – useful for quick profile glances or connecting on LinkedIn.  
  - *Facebook URL, Instagram URL, Company LinkedIn URL* – the company’s social profiles for reference.  
  - *Employee Count (Size)* – number or range of employees.  
  - *Rating, Review Count* – you already have these; keep them visible for context.  
  - *Lead Score or Priority* – if you computed a score or simply a tag like “High Priority”, include that.  
  - You might also keep *Phone* (from the original data) handy – sometimes a call can supplement your email outreach, and as one Clay expert noted, the listed phone for an SMB is often the owner’s number in many cases【8†L221-L228】.  

- **Quality Checks:** Scan through the table for any blanks or anomalies. If **owner name or email is missing for a row**, you might decide to exclude that business from your outreach or handle it differently. For instance, if 40 out of 44 have owner emails and 4 do not, you can still keep those 4 in a separate section (maybe try a generic approach or research them further). Marking those with a flag as mentioned helps. Ensure the emails that you have are labeled with their status (valid, risky, etc.) – you can filter out any that came back “invalid” from Debounce (those you would not email). Lead quality is more important than sheer quantity. It’s better to have 40 well-qualified contacts than 44 where a few might be bad data.  
- **Outreach Integration:** With the enriched data ready, you can push it to your outreach tool. Clay allows you to send rows to various CRMs or sequence tools. Before you do, double-check that each row has: (a) a contact name for personalization, (b) a verified email, and (c) any custom info you might want to merge into your messages (like company name, maybe the city or a snippet of info like “# of years in business” if you plan to mention that). The table you built supports this by having all those columns side by side.  
- **Decision-Maker Targeting:** We specifically went after owners and top execs to maximize response rates. Your table should reflect that – almost every email on the list should be a **direct decision-maker email**, not a catch-all. This means your outreach will be hitting the right person. If you see any generic addresses in the Email column, consider if you want to include them or not. It’s okay if you do, but maybe label them. In our process we tried to avoid that except as fallback. Staying focused on the true decision-maker aligns with best practices for cold outreach (higher chance of conversion when you speak to the boss).  

By structuring the table with clear labels and all relevant info, you make it easy to **execute personalized outreach at scale**. For example, you could have a column that generates a custom intro sentence using AI, based on the data (Clay can do this too – e.g., “AI Write Intro” that uses the rating/review or social media info to create a tailored opener). All the groundwork you’ve done with enrichment will feed into such personalization. The final enriched table acts as your single source of truth for the campaign – you can filter, sort, and plug it into an email sequencer confident that each row is a high-quality lead.
