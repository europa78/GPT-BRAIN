### Structure of a Major Scale:

The major scale is constructed using the following sequence of whole and half steps:

**W - W - H - W - W - W - H**

Here's how it applies to each note of the scale:

1. From the first note to the second note: **Whole step**
2. From the second note to the third note: **Whole step**
3. From the third note to the fourth note: **Half step**
4. From the fourth note to the fifth note: **Whole step**
5. From the fifth note to the sixth note: **Whole step**
6. From the sixth note to the seventh note: **Whole step**
7. From the seventh note back to the octave (eighth note, same as the first but higher): **Half step**

### Example with the C Major Scale:

To illustrate, let's use the C Major scale, which is often the first scale taught in music theory because it contains no sharps or flats.

1. C to D: **Whole step**
2. D to E: **Whole step**
3. E to F: **Half step**
4. F to G: **Whole step**
5. G to A: **Whole step**
6. A to B: **Whole step**
7. B to C (next octave): **Half step**

So, the C Major scale is: **C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C**

### Application:

This pattern is the same for any major scale. To construct a major scale starting on any note, you simply follow the same sequence of whole and half steps. This universal pattern is what defines the sound and characteristics of major scales, giving them a distinctly bright and 'happy' sound in comparison to minor scales, which have a different pattern and a more 'sad' or 'melancholic' sound.
