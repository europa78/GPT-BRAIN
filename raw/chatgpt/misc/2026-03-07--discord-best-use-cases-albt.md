## 1. What This Strategy Is Really For

The purpose of this strategy is not to “sell the stock.”  
The purpose is to make **ALBT easier to understand, easier to follow, and easier to trust**.

That is the right objective for a public-company communications program.

If Advisewisely tries to make people excited about the stock itself, the effort can start to look promotional. If instead Advisewisely helps people clearly understand the company’s business, public milestones, products, and investor materials, awareness can grow in a way that is more credible and much safer. That approach fits the SEC’s expectation that material information be distributed broadly and non-exclusively, and it fits FINRA’s expectation that communications be fair, balanced, and not misleading. citeturn0search1turn0search10turn0search2turn0search11

## 2. The Right Way to Create Awareness

The best way to create awareness for ALBT is to build a system that repeatedly does three things:

**First, make official information easy to find.**  
Investors should never have to dig around the internet to understand the company.

**Second, make the company story easy to understand.**  
Most people do not ignore small public companies because they dislike them. They ignore them because they do not understand them.

**Third, make communications consistent.**  
Trust often comes from repetition, clarity, and professionalism more than from excitement.

In simple terms, Advisewisely should help ALBT become a company that people can follow without confusion.

## 3. The Core Message Advisewisely Should Communicate

Advisewisely should help ALBT communicate a message built around four things:

**Who we are**  
A plain-English explanation of the company and what it does.

**What problem we are solving**  
Why the business matters in the real world.

**What progress has already been made publicly**  
Public milestones, released materials, filings, presentations, and official announcements.

**Why investors should keep following the company**  
Not because of hype, but because the company is communicating clearly and consistently.

The tone should always feel:
- factual,
- calm,
- confident,
- easy to understand,
- grounded in public information.

The message should never feel like:
- a stock pitch,
- a promise,
- a prediction,
- a rush,
- or a campaign to create trading activity.

FINRA’s published standards explicitly warn against false, misleading, exaggerated, unwarranted, or promissory claims, and against projecting performance in public communications. citeturn0search2turn0search14turn0search5

It should build a **stack**.

**1. Investor Relations page**  
This should remain the main source of truth. It should clearly link to SEC filings, press releases, investor presentations, webcast replays, and official contact information. **2. Press releases and SEC filings**  
These are the foundation. Social content should point back to them, not replace them.

**3. LinkedIn**  
Best for professional credibility, management visibility, industry context, and company updates in a business tone.

**4. X**  
Best for quick links to public announcements, live event reminders, and replay distribution.

**5. YouTube**  
Best for management clips, public product explainers, webcast replays, and interview-style content.

**6. Discord**  
Best as a secondary education hub, not the main promotional engine.

The official IR page and disclosed official social channels should work together. The SEC’s guidance is clear that social media can be used, but investors need to know which channels are official, and disclosure obligations do not go away just because the format is social. citeturn0search1turn0search10

## 5. How Discord Should Be Used

Discord should be used like a **digital investor education room**.

That means it should help people:
- find official public materials,
- understand the business,
- ask basic questions,
- follow public events,
- stay organized.

It should **not** operate like:
- a stock chat room,
- a trading-alert channel,
- a “community army,”
- a place for price targets,
- or a place for excitement around market movement.

### The best Discord structure for ALBT

**Start Here**
- Welcome
- Rules and disclaimer
- Official links

**Official Information**
- Announcements
- SEC filings
- Press releases
- Events and webcasts

**Education**
- About ALBT
- Products and services
- FAQ
- Industry background

**Community**
- General discussion
- Questions for team
- Public-news discussion

### Important note

No technical-analysis channel.  
No price-talk channel.  
No “why the stock is undervalued” content.  
No community encouragement to spread the story.

That keeps the server aligned with trust-building, not hype-building.

## 6. What to Post Each Week

Advisewisely should use a steady weekly rhythm.

### Monday: This Week at ALBT
A clean summary of:
- upcoming public events,
- links to recent public materials,
- key reminders.

### Tuesday: Company Education Post
A plain-English explainer on:
- what ALBT does,
- how the business works,
- what problem it solves,
- what a public milestone means.

### Wednesday: Public Q&A
Answer one or two recurring questions using only information already published publicly.

### Thursday: Content Spotlight
Highlight one already-public item:
- a press release,
- investor slide,
- public demo,
- webcast clip,
- management quote from an official event.

### Friday: Weekly Recap
Summarize everything ALBT publicly released or discussed during the week and link back to official sources.

### Monthly
Publish:
- one deeper management interview,
- one investor FAQ refresh,
- one “state of the company” educational summary based entirely on public materials.

This kind of cadence builds familiarity without looking forced.

## 7. What Never to Post

This is where most public-company social efforts go wrong.

Advisewisely should never post:
- direct stock recommendations,
- buy/sell language,
- price targets,
- “big move coming” language,
- “watch this closely” language tied to the stock,
- statements about unusual trading,
- material information before broad public dissemination,
- exaggerated claims,
- selective answers that some investors get and others do not.

That is not just a style issue. It is a legal and reputational issue. The SEC’s Reg FD guidance is built around broad, non-exclusive dissemination of material information, and FINRA’s communications standards emphasize fair, balanced, and non-misleading content. citeturn0search1turn0search10turn0search2turn0search11

## 8. The Internal Approval Process

A good strategy is only as strong as its controls.

Advisewisely should recommend this process for ALBT:

**Step 1: Draft**  
Write the post in plain English.

**Step 2: Source-check**  
Match every important statement to a public filing, press release, webcast, or already-public presentation.

**Step 3: Risk screen**  
Check for:
- hype language,
- predictions,
- one-sided framing,
- implied promises,
- selective disclosure risk.

**Step 4: Approval**  
Have a designated reviewer approve anything that touches:
- financial performance,
- partnerships,
- product milestones,
- management commentary,
- investor questions.

**Step 5: Archive**  
Keep a clean internal record of what was posted and when.

That kind of workflow helps keep communications consistent and defensible.

## 9. How to Measure Success

The success metrics should not be built around trading behavior.

They should be built around **attention quality** and **understanding**.

The best metrics are:
- IR page visits,
- time spent on investor materials,
- webcast registrations,
- replay views,
- FAQ engagement,
- Discord member retention,
- quality of investor questions,
- LinkedIn engagement from relevant audiences,
- media pickup of public announcements,
- growth in followers across official channels.

If the quality of the audience improves and more people are consuming official materials, the strategy is working.
