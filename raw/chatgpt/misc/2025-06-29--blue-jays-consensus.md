**GPT:** Here’s a roundup of the leading modern baseball prediction/projection systems in use today:

1. **PECOTA (Player Empirical Comparison and Optimization Test Algorithm)**  
   - Developed by Baseball Prospectus, PECOTA uses historical “nearest-neighbor” comparables plus regression trees and aging curves to forecast player performance and team wins.

2. **ZiPS (Oliver ZIPS), by Dan Szymborski (FanGraphs)**  
   - A weighted, aging-curve-adjusted regression model that blends past stats with similarity scores to project hitters and pitchers for the upcoming season.

3. **Steamer Projections (SAAM/Steamer)**  
   - Originally built by Jared Cross, Steamer combines trending in a player’s most recent three years, regression toward mean, and aging curves, with explicit park-and-league adjustments.

4. **Marcel (Bill James)**  
   - A very simple three-year weighted average with regression-to-the-mean and minimal aging adjustment—often used as a “baseline” to gauge the value of more complex systems.

5. **FanGraphs Depth Charts (FGDC)**  
   - An aggregate of BPA (Blend of Projections and Analytics) used to model each team’s likely lineup and rotation, then run through a runs‐created simulation to estimate wins.

6. **FiveThirtyEight’s MLB Elo**  
   - A dynamic rating system that updates team ratings based on game results, margin of victory, and home-field, then converts those ratings into win probabilities and playoff odds.

7. **SIERA (Skill‐Interactive ERA)**  
   - A defense‐independent pitching metric that models run prevention better than traditional ERA or FIP, often used as an input to projection systems.

8. **DRA & DRC+ (Deserved Run Average / Deserved Runs Created)**  
   - Baseball Prospectus’s in‐depth metrics that strip out luck and defense, feeding a more “true talent” view of pitchers and hitters for forecasting.

9. **Collider’s Neural‐Net & Random-Forest Models**  
   - A growing class of machine‐learning approaches that combine Statcast data (exit velocity, launch angle, spin, etc.) with historical outcomes to predict batter and pitcher performance.

10. **Baseball-Reference’s Marcel + Similarity Scores**  
    - Baseball-Reference offers a Marcel baseline plus a “similarity‐score” adjustment that functions as a straightforward, transparent projection.

11. **BP2 (Baseball Prospectus’s PythagenPat & Tempo Adjustments)**  
    - Uses Pythagorean theorem variants and park-time adjusters to translate runs scored/allowed into expected win totals.

12. **Dynamic In-Game Win‐Probability Models (MLB Statcast, Baseball Savant)**  
    - Real‐time algorithms that update each team’s chance of winning based on inning, score, base/runners, count, and even pitch‐level data.

**GPT:** Beyond traditional box-score stats like batting average (BA), on-base percentage (OBP), and runs batted in (RBI), today’s analysts lean heavily on rate- and context-adjusted metrics. Here’s a rundown by category:

---

## Hitting  
- **Slugging Percentage (SLG)**  
  Total bases ÷ at-bats; weights extra-base hits.  
- **On-Base Plus Slugging (OPS)**  
  OBP + SLG; a shorthand for overall offensive value.  
- **Weighted On-Base Average (wOBA)**  
  Assigns proper run values to each event (BB, H, 2B, HR, etc.), then scales to OBP units.  
- **Weighted Runs Created Plus (wRC+)**  
  Team- and park-adjusted version of wOBA, scaled so 100 = league average (every point = 1% above/below).  
- **Isolated Power (ISO)**  
  SLG – BA; measures raw extra-base power.  
- **Batting Average on Balls In Play (BABIP)**  
  (H – HR) ÷ (AB – K – HR + SF); gauges luck or quality of contact.  
- **Walk Rate (BB%) & Strikeout Rate (K%)**  
  BB ÷ PA; K ÷ PA—key process metrics.  
- **Barrel % / Hard-Hit % / Exit Velocity / Launch Angle**  
  Statcast-driven measures of contact quality.  

## Pitching  
- **Fielding Independent Pitching (FIP)**  
  Estimates ERA solely from K, BB, HBP, HR, neutralizing defense.  
- **Expected FIP (xFIP)**  
  Replaces actual HR rate with league-average HR/FB to “normalize” HR luck.  
- **Skill-Interactive ERA (SIERA)**  
  A refined, batted-ball-aware FIP variant that better predicts future run prevention.  
- **Walk Rate (BB%) & Strikeout Rate (K%)**  
  As with hitters, BB ÷ PA and K ÷ PA for pitchers.  
- **Home-Run per Fly-Ball Rate (HR/FB)**  
  Measures susceptibility to allowing homers.  
- **Velocity & Spin Rate**  
  Statcast-driven pitch-profiling stats that correlate with future K% and HR rates.  

## Fielding  
- **Ultimate Zone Rating (UZR)**  
  Estimates runs saved or cost vs. an average defender, based on batted-ball location.  
- **Defensive Runs Saved (DRS)**  
  Broadly similar to UZR—runs above/below average.  
- **Outs Above Average (OAA)**  
  Statcast’s location-based DEF metric, measuring likelihood of making each play.  

## Base Running  
- **BsR (Base-running Runs)**  
  FanGraphs’ composite metric for stolen bases, taking extra bases, and other running events.  
- **Ultimate Base-running (UBR)**  
  Baseball-Reference’s break down of non-SBA (stolen base attempts) running value.  

## Win-Probability & Leverage  
- **Win Probability Added (WPA)**  
  Change in a team’s win probability before and after each PA.  
- **Leverage Index (LI)**  
  Measures pressure of a PA (high­-leverage situations amplify WPA).

By combining these rate, context, and process metrics, modern projections and in-game models gain far more predictive power than raw counts like RBI or HR alone.

**Mike:** How do professional odds makers set the line and point spreads ? What methods do they use ?

**GPT:** Oddsmakers blend rigorous data science with market savvy and risk management to turn predictive insights into a live betting line. Here’s a step-by-step look at how the process works:

---

## 1. Build a Power-Rating or Predictive Model  
Before a line ever appears on the board, pros have a “power rating” for every team (or player) that quantifies its true strength. Common approaches include:  
- **Elo or TrueSkill‐style ratings** that update after each game based on outcome and margin.  
- **Regression models** (linear, ridge, lasso) trained on historical game margins and inputs like offensive/defensive efficiency, pace, home/away splits, rest days, injuries, weather, etc.  
- **Simulation frameworks** (Monte Carlo): you simulate the season or individual contests thousands of times using distributions (e.g. Poisson for runs or scores) to get win probabilities and expected margins.  

These systems all produce an **expected margin of victory** (E[M]) for Team A vs. Team B.

---

## 2. Convert Margin to a Betting Spread  
Once you have E[M], you translate it into a point spread or run line by:  
1. **Rounding** to the nearest half-point (to avoid pushes).  
2. **Embedding the vig (juice)**: rather than offering 50–50 on either side, you skew payout odds so the “fair” 50% line becomes something like –110 on both sides.  
3. **Applying market conventions** (e.g. in the NBA home team often gets a ~3-point edge; in NFL, 2.5–3).  

That gives you the **initial** line (often called the “opening number”).

---

## 3. Factor in Sharps & Market Intelligence  
Before publishing, sportsbooks compare their line to:  
- **Other books and exchanges (Betfair, Pinnacle)** to gauge consensus.  
- **Sharp action and syndicate reports**—if early pro bets already start hammering one side, the initial line may shift pre‐market.  
- **News overlays** for late scratches, weather forecasts, motivational factors.  

Often a manual trader will tweak the algorithmic line up or down to reflect what they know about early money flow.

---

## 4. Risk & Liability Management (Line Movement)  
Once live, the line becomes a tool to balance the book:  
- If too much money comes in on one side, the book is exposed to large liability if that side wins.  
- To encourage bets on the other side, oddsmakers will **move the spread** (or adjust ML odds) so that the unpopular side becomes more attractive.  
- They continuously monitor bet size, ticket count, and where sharp vs. public money is going.  

The goal isn’t necessarily to predict the “true” outcome perfectly—it’s to get roughly equal dollars on both sides so the sportsbook locks in its vig.

---

## 5. In-Play Adjustments & Futures  
For live betting, the same power ratings and Poisson/Skellam simulators run continuously with updated game-state inputs (score, inning/quarter, time remaining, base/runners, possession). That churns out new win probabilities, which are then converted instantaneously into in-play spreads and totals.

For season-long futures (like win totals or championships), books use those same simulations across the full schedule, then adjust for known/off-season rosters and major market expectations.

---

### Key Tools & Techniques in the Toolbox  
- **Linear & logistic regression** for margin and ML probabilities  
- **Elo/TrueSkill** for an always-updating power metric  
- **Monte Carlo simulation** using Poisson (scores/runs) or negative-binomial (goals) distributions  
- **Machine-learning models** (random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets) on high-dimensional features (advanced stats, tracking data)  
- **Automated pipelines + trader overlays** so human experts can override or fine-tune when needed  
- **Kelly-based risk sizing** internally to gauge how much a sharper’s bet should move the line  

---

By combining these quantitative engines with real-time market feedback and seasoned trader judgment, professional oddsmakers craft lines that both reflect the “true” edge and manage their financial exposure.

1. **Betting-Market Conversion & Run-Expectancy**

They then invert Bill James’ Pythagorean Expectation (ExWP = RS^γ / (RS^γ + RA^γ), with γ≈1.75 in MLB) to solve for each team’s expected runs scored (RS) and allowed (RA) from the market odds and total runs line citeturn4view0turn2search6.

2. **Linear Regression for Offense & Defense Ratings**  
   Those expected RS/RA feed into a linear regression across all games in a rolling look-back window (typically 4 weeks for MLB).  The regression solves for one “offense” variable per team and one “defense” variable per starting pitcher (treating each SP as its own defensive profile), yielding granular ratings for runs scored and runs allowed citeturn4view0.  
   - **Home-Field Adjustment:** A fixed bump (~+0.2 runs added to a home team’s expectation, –0.2 to the visitor) corrects for the ~54% home win rate in baseball citeturn4view0.

3. **Strength-of-Schedule & RPI**  
   To capture raw win–loss context, many models incorporate a classic Relative Power Index (RPI):  
   > RPI = 0.25 × WP + 0.50 × OWP + 0.25 × OOWP  
   where WP is team winning %, OWP is opponents’ average WP, and OOWP is opponents’ opponents’ WP citeturn2search6.  
   Separately, Elo- or TrueSkill-based systems update each team’s rating after every game using the actual outcome and score margin—often applying a logarithmic margin weight to maintain transitivity across the league citeturn7search1.

4. **Sabermetric & Contextual Inputs**  
   Many sportsbooks overlay their market/regression frameworks with team-level aggregates of advanced metrics and situational factors:  
   - **Offensive Metrics:**  
     - wRC+ (park- and league-adjusted weighted Runs Created; 100 = league average) citeturn9search0  
   - **Pitching Metrics:**  
     - FIP (Defense-Independent Pitching: HR, K, BB, HBP only) citeturn8search10  
     - xFIP (regresses HR to league average HR/FB rate) citeturn8search1

   - **Defensive & Baserunning:** UZR/DRS for fielding, BsR for base-running value  
   - **Contextual Adjusters:** Park factors, days of rest, travel/time-zone shifts, weather, injury news  

Together, these components produce dynamic “power ratings” that reflect true team strength, current form, and market sentiment—forming the quantitative backbone of modern baseball betting lines.

## 1. Poisson‐Based Models  
**Single‐Team Poisson**  
- Assumes runs scored by Team A and Team B are independent Poisson random variables with means λ₁ and λ₂.  
- Total runs ∼ Poisson(λ₁ + λ₂).  
- Widely used because of its simplicity and the “rare event” nature of runs per inning citeturn1search2.

---

## 2. Overdispersed Count Models  
**Negative Binomial**  
- Generalizes Poisson by adding a dispersion parameter (r,p), decoupling mean and variance.  
- Captures extra variability (“big innings” or offense surges) that Poisson underestimates citeturn0search3.

---

## 3. Bivariate Poisson Models  
- Models runs by both teams jointly, allowing a covariance term to reflect correlation (e.g. weather, base‐state effects).  
- Better fits margins when scores co‐move; common in advanced soccer/basketball analytics and being adapted to baseball citeturn0search1.

---

## 4. Conway–Maxwell–Poisson (CMP)  
- Introduces a dispersion parameter ν to the Poisson:  
  P(X=k) ∝ λᵏ / (k!ᵛ).  
- Flexibly handles both under‐ and overdispersion without switching to a different family                                                                                                                             citeturn1search3.

---

## 5. Weibull‐Derived Run Models  
- Assumes each team’s runs per game follow a Weibull distribution; independent draws yield a total‐runs density via convolution.  
- Underpins the theoretical justification for Pythagorean‐expectation exponent (~1.83) and can be inverted to model totals citeturn1academia11.

---

## 6. Monte Carlo Simulation  
- Draw thousands of season or single‐game outcomes using one of the above distributions (Poisson, NB, Weibull, etc.), then record the empirical total‐runs distribution.  
- Easily incorporates park factors, weather, rest, bullpen usage, and late scratches.

---

## 7. Machine‐Learning & Regression Approaches  
- **Gradient Boosting / Random Forests / Neural Nets** trained on high-dimensional features (team pace, Statcast metrics, bullpen leverage, weather forecasts).  
- Predict the probability Total > X directly (e.g. via logistic regression), with X spanning all plausible totals.

---

## 8. Composite & Hybrid Systems  
- Many shops blend a “market-implied” distribution (reverse‐engineered from other books’ lines) with a “fundamentals” distribution (from the models above), weighting each by recent sharp‐money flows.  

---

**GPT:** You’ve posted the general‐purpose asymptotic and exact formulas for the Conway–Maxwell–Poisson (CMP) distribution—a two-parameter extension of the Poisson that can flexibly handle over- or under-dispersion in count data.  On its face, these formulas (for cumulants κₙ, standardized moments γ₁/γ₂, factorial moments, etc.) tell you how to compute means, variances, skewness, etc., once you’ve chosen your λ (mean-scale) and ν (dispersion) parameters.  But by themselves they don’t “predict” baseball scores—you have to:

---

## 1. Fit λ and ν to Historical Run Totals  
Pick a window of past games (per team, per ballpark, per pitcher, whatever slice you like) and estimate λ and ν by maximum‐likelihood or method-of-moments.  For example:  
- Use the CMP mean and variance expansions  
  \[
    E[X]\;=\;\lambda^{1/\nu}\Bigl(1+\tfrac{1-\nu}{2\nu}\,\lambda^{-1/\nu}+\cdots\Bigr),\quad
    \mathrm{Var}[X]\;=\;\lambda^{1/\nu}\Bigl(1+\cdots\Bigr)
  \]  
  (or the exact integer-ν formulas involving Bessel or hypergeometric functions) to solve for λ and ν from your sample’s empirical mean and variance.  

---

## 2. Generate a Run‐Scoring Distribution  
Once λ,ν are fitted, the CMP pmf  
\[
  P(X=k) \;=\;\frac{\lambda^k}{(k!)^\nu}\,\Biggl[\sum_{j=0}^\infty\frac{\lambda^j}{(j!)^\nu}\Biggr]^{-1}
\]  
gives you each team’s probability of scoring k runs.  (You don’t actually need all those cumulant expansions to compute P(X=k)—you just evaluate the normalizing constant Z(λ,ν) numerically.)

---

## 3. Turn Runs into Win Probabilities  
With two independent CMP run distributions, \(X\sim\mathrm{CMP}(\lambda_A,\nu_A)\) for Team A and \(Y\sim\mathrm{CMP}(\lambda_B,\nu_B)\) for Team B, you can approximate  
\[
  P(A\text{ wins}) \;=\;\sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^{k-1}P(X=k)\,P(Y=j).
\]  
Or you can Monte-Carlo-simulate thousands of games by drawing \((X,Y)\) pairs from each CMP and tallying A > B, A = B, A < B.

---

## 4. Embed Contextual Adjusters  
Just like Poisson or Negative-Binomial models, you’d then layer in park factors, home-field runs bumps, bullpen/rest adjustments, weather, lineup changes, etc., by modulating λ (and possibly ν) before recomputing your CMP distributions.

---

## 5. Compare to the Market & Produce a Line  
Finally you translate your model’s P(A wins), or your simulated run-differential distribution, into a moneyline or run-line by the usual methods (inverting Pythagorean expectation, rounding to half-runs, embedding vig).

---

### Bottom Line  
- **Yes**: in principle you can use the CMP formulas to build a more flexible “runs per game” model than plain Poisson.  
- **But**: the heavy asymptotic cumulant expansions you quoted are mostly of theoretical interest (they let you approximate high-order moments when λ is large).  In practice you’d fit λ,ν numerically (MLE or method of moments), compute the CMP pmf directly, then simulate win probabilities.  

Most pro shops stick with Poisson or Negative‐Binomial (simpler to fit and compute), but a CMP approach can capture non-Poisson dispersion in “runs scored” and—once embedded as above—can feed seamlessly into your game‐outcome predictions.

## Algorithm: CMP Game‐Outcome Model

1. **Input**  
   - Moneyline odds: \(\mathrm{ML}_A\), \(\mathrm{ML}_B\)  
   - Total-runs line: \(R_{\rm tot}\)  
   - (Optionally) choose a dispersion parameter \(\nu\). If you don’t have data to fit \(\nu\), set \(\nu=1\) (Poisson).  

2. **Convert odds → “Fair” win probabilities**  
   1. Convert American ML to raw implied probabilities:  
      \[
        q_A = 
        \begin{cases}
          \dfrac{100}{\mathrm{ML}_A + 100}, & \mathrm{ML}_A>0,\\[6pt]
          \dfrac{-\mathrm{ML}_A}{-\mathrm{ML}_A + 100}, & \mathrm{ML}_A<0,
        \end{cases}
        \quad
        q_B = 1-q_A_{\rm second}
      \]  
   2. Remove vig by normalizing:  
      \[
        p_A = \frac{q_A}{\,q_A + q_B\,},\quad
        p_B = 1-p_A.
      \]

3. **Back‐solve Pythagorean expectation → expected runs**  
   We assume each team’s expected runs \(x\) and \(y\) satisfy  
   \[
     x + y \;=\; R_{\rm tot}, 
     \quad
     \frac{x^{\gamma}}{\,x^{\gamma} + y^{\gamma}\,} \;=\; p_A,
   \]
   where \(\gamma\approx1.83\) for MLB.  Numerically solve for \(x\) (Team A’s expected runs), then set \(y = R_{\rm tot}-x\).

4. **Set CMP parameters**  
   \[
     \lambda_A = x^{\nu},\quad
     \lambda_B = y^{\nu}.
   \]
   The CMP pmf is then
   \[
     P_A(k) \;=\; \frac{\lambda_A^k}{(k!)^\nu}\;\Bigl[\sum_{j=0}^\infty\frac{\lambda_A^j}{(j!)^\nu}\Bigr]^{-1},
     \quad 
     P_B(j)\;=\;\text{(same with }\lambda_B\!).
   \]

5. **Compute model win–prob**  
   \[
     \widehat{P}(A\!>\!B)
     \;=\;\sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^{k-1} P_A(k)\,P_B(j).
   \]
   (In practice truncate at \(k,j\le 15\) or MC-simulate.)

6. **Compare to market**  
   - If \(\widehat{P}(A\!>\!B) - p_A > 0\), you’ve found an edge on A.  
   - If \(\widehat{P}(A\!>\!B) - p_A < 0\), no value on A (or look at B).

## Worked Example: Twins @ Tigers (End 2nd)

1. **Inputs from card**  
   - ML Twins = +350, ML Tigers = –500  
   - Total runs = 6.5  
   - Choose \(\nu=1\) (Poisson) for simplicity.

2. **Raw implied**  
   \[
     q_{\rm Twins}=\frac{100}{350+100}=0.222,\quad
     q_{\rm Tigers}=\frac{500}{500+100}=0.833.
   \]

3. **Remove vig**  
   \[
     p_{\rm Twins}=\frac{0.222}{0.222+0.833}\approx0.211,\quad
     p_{\rm Tigers}=0.789.
   \]

4. **Back-solve runs** (\(\gamma=1.83\))  
   Solve 
   \(\;x^{1.83}/(x^{1.83}+(6.5-x)^{1.83})=0.211,\;x+y=6.5\).  
   ⇒  
   \[
     x\approx2.13\quad(\text{Twins}),\quad
     y\approx4.37\quad(\text{Tigers}).
   \]

5. **CMP params** (\(\nu=1\))  
   \[
     \lambda_{\rm Twins}=2.13,\quad
     \lambda_{\rm Tigers}=4.37.
   \]

6. **Model win-prob** (Poisson proxy)  
   \[
     \widehat P(\text{Twins win})
     =\sum_{k=0}^{15}\sum_{j=0}^{k-1}
       \mathrm{Pois}(k;2.13)\,\mathrm{Pois}(j;4.37)
     \approx0.135.
   \]

7. **Edge check**  
   \[
     \widehat{P}-p_{\rm Twins}\approx0.135-0.211=-0.076.
   \]
   No positive edge on the Twins ML (+350).
