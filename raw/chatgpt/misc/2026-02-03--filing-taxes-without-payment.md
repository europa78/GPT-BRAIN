**Mike:** can I file taxes so I have proof I filed them without paying what I owe ?

**GPT:** Yes — in most cases you can **file your tax return on time even if you can’t pay what you owe**, and you’ll still have **proof you filed**.

### What “proof I filed” usually means
You’ll get **official confirmation** that the IRS accepted your return:

- **E-file (best):** you get an **IRS e-file acceptance** message/receipt from your tax software or preparer (save/print it).
- **Mail:** you can prove filing by mailing **Certified Mail + Return Receipt** (or using an approved private delivery service) and keeping copies.

### Important: filing vs. paying
- **Filing** is submitting the return.
- **Paying** is sending the money.
They are separate. You can file without full payment.

### What happens if you file but don’t pay?
You generally avoid the big penalty for not filing, but you may still owe:
- **Failure-to-pay penalty** (usually smaller than failure-to-file)
- **Interest** on the unpaid balance  
So filing is usually the right move if you’re short on cash.

### Options to file now and pay later
After (or while) you file, you can usually choose one of these:

1. **Pay what you can now**  
   Even a partial payment reduces penalties/interest.

2. **Installment plan (payment plan)**  
   You can apply online and pay monthly.

3. **Short-term extension to pay**  
   The IRS sometimes allows extra time to pay in full (still accrues interest, but avoids some setup fees).

4. **Request penalty relief (if you qualify)**  
   Some people qualify for “first-time abate” or other relief.

### Do NOT confuse these two
- **Filing extension** (Form 4868) gives you **more time to file**, **not** more time to pay.  
  If you extend, you still should pay as much as you can by the deadline to reduce penalties/interest.

### Fast checklist to get solid proof
- File **electronically** if possible.
- Save:
  - the **accepted** confirmation,
  - a PDF copy of the return,
  - and any payment plan confirmation (if you set one up).

### One caution
If you already got IRS letters or you’re dealing with back taxes/collections, the best move can change (still usually file ASAP, but strategy matters).
