These firms allow traders to demonstrate their skills in a simulated environment before providing them with access to real funds. Here's how the process generally works:

### 1. **Evaluation Phase:**
   - **Demo Trading:** Traders must first go through an evaluation period, often called the Combine (Topstep’s term). This is a simulated trading environment where traders must meet specific profit targets, risk management rules, and consistency requirements.
   - **Rules and Targets:** During the evaluation, traders must adhere to a set of rules such as daily loss limits, maximum drawdowns, and position size limits. For example, a trader might need to make $5,000 in profits while not losing more than $1,000 in a day.
   - **Costs:** Traders usually pay a monthly fee to participate in the evaluation phase, which covers the firm's costs for data, technology, and evaluation services.

### 2. **Funded Account Phase:**
   - **Access to Capital:** Once the trader passes the evaluation, they receive access to a funded trading account. The size of this account depends on the evaluation performance and the selected plan.
   - **Profit Split:** Traders keep a percentage of the profits they generate, often around 80-90%, while the firm takes the remainder. This split is the firm’s incentive for providing the capital and managing risk.
   - **Risk Parameters:** Even in a funded account, traders must adhere to risk management rules. If a trader violates these rules, such as exceeding the maximum drawdown, they can lose their funded status.

### 3. **Scaling Plan:**
   - **Increasing Buying Power:** Some firms offer a scaling plan where traders can increase their buying power and access more capital as they demonstrate consistency and risk management in their trading.
   - **Additional Evaluations:** Traders might need to hit additional profit targets or pass further evaluations to scale up their account size.

### 4. **Fees and Costs:**
   - **Monthly Fees:** Most firms charge monthly fees during the evaluation phase. There are usually no ongoing costs once traders are funded, though some firms may charge for data or software.
   - **Withdrawal Fees:** There may be fees associated with withdrawing profits, depending on the firm’s policies.

### 5. **Benefits and Risks:**
   - **Benefits:** The main benefit is access to capital without risking personal funds, allowing traders to grow their skills and potentially earn profits in a live market setting.
   - **Risks:** Traders must adhere to strict risk management rules, and failure to do so can result in losing funded status. Also, the evaluation fees can add up if multiple attempts are required.

Funded trading programs are ideal for skilled traders who want to trade with more capital than they have personally but are willing to accept the costs and restrictions that come with the evaluation process.

### **Why Funded Accounts Typically Avoid Stocks:**
1. **High Capital Requirements:** Trading stocks often requires significantly more capital due to lower leverage compared to futures. For example, leveraging a $100,000 stock account might only allow trading up to $200,000 worth of stock, while futures offer much higher leverage ratios.
   
2. **Pattern Day Trading (PDT) Rule:** In the U.S., the PDT rule requires a minimum account balance of $25,000 to day trade stocks frequently. This rule does not apply to futures, making them more accessible for day traders.

3. **Lower Profit Margins for Firms:** Stock trading can involve higher transaction costs, including SEC fees, routing fees, and exchange fees, which reduce profit margins for funded trading firms.

4. **Complex Risk Management:** Stocks can have more complex risk profiles due to company-specific risks, earnings announcements, and news-driven volatility, making it harder for firms to manage overall exposure.

### **Programs Offering Stock Trading:**
Some funded trading firms are beginning to expand their offerings to include stocks, but these are less common and often come with specific conditions:
   
- **Prop Trading Firms (Prop Shops):** Unlike Topstep, some prop trading firms do provide access to stocks, but they typically require traders to work on-site or go through a more stringent evaluation and training process.
   
- **Stock CFDs (Contracts for Difference):** Some programs offer CFDs on stocks, allowing traders to speculate on stock price movements without owning the underlying asset. CFDs offer similar leverage and lower capital requirements but are not allowed in the U.S.

- **Dedicated Stock Trading Programs:** A few funded trading firms (e.g., Fidelcrest, FTMO) offer stock trading through evaluation accounts, but they typically have different rules and risk management requirements compared to futures.

### **Key Points for Stock Traders:**
