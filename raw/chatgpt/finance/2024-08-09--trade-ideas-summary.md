The following screen isn't related to the headlines about companies "beating" or "missing" consensus estimates among analysts working for brokerage firms. Instead, the screen focuses on sales growth from a year earlier, with two quality filters that highlight pricing power and efficiency.
Earnings-season stock screen
We are focused on financial results for the second quarter, but about 20% of companies in the S&P 500 SPX have fiscal years that don't match the calendar.
Through Thursday, 453 of the companies had reported results for fiscal quarters ending  May 25  or later.
We screened the 453 companies for the following:
Increased quarterly sales per share from a year earlier. We looked at the per-share figures because these would reflect dilution to shareholders if a company issued stock to raise money. Conversely, sales per share would rise if a company lowered its share count by repurchasing stock.Improved gross profit margins. A company's gross margin is its net sales, less the cost of goods or services sold, divided by sales. Net sales are sales minus returns and discounts, such as coupons. The cost of goods or services sold includes the actual expenses when making the items or providing the services. Gross margin is a measurement of pricing power. A combination of an expanding gross margin and increasing sales is a good sign. Improved operating margins. The operating margin incorporates more overhead and other expenses that aren't directly related to the production of goods and services. It can be summarized as earnings before interest and taxes, divided by sales.
Increasing sales rapidly while improving gross and operating margins might be particularly impressive during a period of upward pressure on wages and other rising input costs.
Profit margins vary by industry, so high margins alone might not make for good comparisons between companies. They might be most useful for comparisons within peer or industry groups.
We didn't look at earnings per share in the screen because one-time events and accounting adjustments can distort bottom-line profit figures.
Among the 453 companies, gross margin and operating margin figures were available from FactSet for 407 companies. These figures typically aren't available for companies in the financial sector because banks and insurers have their own industry-based profitability metrics.
For some companies, FactSet cannot calculate the margins until more detailed quarterly 10-Q reports are filed with the  Securities and Exchange Commission . Sometimes these aren't available until several weeks after a company files its earnings press release.
