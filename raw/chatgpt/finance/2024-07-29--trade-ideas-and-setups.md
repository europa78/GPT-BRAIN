A rate cut isn't expected in the coming week, but the Fed could signal if small-caps can keep up their winning streak

A market-moving force could be about to hit a rapidly moving stock market.

U.S. equities were extra jittery in the past week as investors navigated a sharp selloff in big-tech names and a rotation into small-cap and value stocks. This movement from large to small has been one of the main market trends in July, enough to gain its own nickname - the "Great Rotation."

In many ways, the shift was bound to happen. So-called Magnificent Seven stocks had a pretty spectacular start to the year, with shares of Nvidia Corp. Meanwhile, the Russell 2000, RUT which tracks small-cap stocks, gained 10.2% over the past 12 trading days, as of market close on July 26. It outperformed the S&P 500 by 13.3%, which was its largest 12-day outperformance of the S&P 500 of all time, according to Dow Jones Market Data. "I think the challenge is that the earnings weren't bad, but they had to be amazing in order to justify the run-up that we saw in prices," Emily Roland, co-chief investment strategist at John Hancock Investment Management, told MarketWatch.

Still, the momentum of this trend could hinge on next week's big event - the July FOMC meeting.

What to listen for in the Fed meeting

The Federal Open Market Committee has been scheduled to meet on July 30 and 31. While Fed officials are not expected to cut rates at the meeting, investors will be paying attention to get a better idea of when the Fed might start cutting interest rates.

Rate increases and cuts can be a blunt hammer when it comes to the economy, and they affect sectors of the economy in different ways. This has been especially true for small-cap versus large-cap companies, because smaller companies tend to be more reliant on business loans to keep operating and growing.

"People typically believe that when you get rate cuts that benefits small-caps in a couple of ways," Thomas Martin, senior portfolio manager at GLOBALT Investments, told MarketWatch. "Small-cap companies are more sensitive to variable- rate financing, to floating rates, to shorter-term rates."

Cutting interest rates means loans taken out by small companies can get cheaper, thus their operating expenses should go down, which can benefit earnings, Martin said. There also can be the added benefit of lower rates stimulating the economy, he said, which can mean customers have the capacity to spend more money too.

Investors started moving from megacap stocks to small-cap stocks before the first rate cut of this cycle, which means small-caps could build even more momentum when the Fed does start lowering rates. Martin thinks the Great Rotation could continue, at least to an extent.

"It's a recalibration and it gets things back to a more reasonable relationships among the various parts [of the market]. There's probably some more to go," Martin told MarketWatch. "I don't think it goes any further than you want to be diversified and have exposure to a lot of different places. I don't think it means you go from being all growth to all value."

This may have to do with the double-edge sword that is cutting rates. Lower interest rates would likely stimulate the economy, but the act of lowering rates in itself could signal that the economy needs to be stimulated. Is the Fed cutting rates because it won the fight against inflation, or because high interest rates are pushing the economy in the wrong direction? Investors will be paying close attention to how Fed Chair Powell answers this question.

Americans' perception of the stock market and the U.S. economy are unusually split

Investors nowadays understandably are asking questions such as: "Is the economy in good shape or not?" "How bad is inflation?" And, "How is the stock market performing, really?"

The answer: The U.S. stock market and the U.S. economy are unusually split right now - and that suggests a bear market and a recession are likely.

One indicator that captures this polarization is the spread between what consumers believe about the economy in general and how they rate their immediate financial prospects. A benchmark that represents the former is the Conference Board's Consumer Confidence Index (CCI), while the latter is represented by University of Michigan's Index of Consumer Sentiment (UMICS). Hardly ever over the past 45 years has the CCI been so much higher than the UMICS as it is now, as you can see from the accompanying chart.

As you can also see from the chart, each economic recession since 1979 has begun soon after this spread rose above 20. If the future is like the past, that means a recession is now imminent, since the spread hit an all-time high of 49.3 in late 2022. The spread has narrowed somewhat since then, but as of June it was still higher than 91% of all other monthly readings since 1979 (when both the UMICS and the CCI began being reported on a monthly basis).

Furthermore, early indications are that the July 2024 CCI-UMICS spread will be even wider than June's. We won't know for sure until the Conference Board releases its latest CCI index on July 30 - but the University of Michigan just released the preliminary July reading of its Index of Consumer Sentiment, and it fell to an eight-month low.

What accounts for the large spread?

The reason to focus on this wide gulf between different investor perspectives is not to take sides. Each is valid - since for many the economy is running on all cylinders, and yet for many others their standard of living hasn't even kept up with inflation.

There undoubtedly are numerous individual factors that have conspired to cause these split perspectives. Douglas Gerlach discussed a number of them in a recent thought piece entitled "Two Economies and Two Stock Markets." Gerlach is editor of the Investor Advisory Service newsletter, which is published by the National Association of Investors (the national association of investment clubs).

Here are some of the dimensions that Gerlach mentioned along which investors have divergent perspectives:

Stock ownership: "An investor with a substantial stock portfolio considers [economic] conditions to be pretty good," he wrote. "Someone who doesn't own stocks might not share that rosy point of view." According to the Federal Reserve's latest Survey of Consumer Finances, 58% of American households own stocks - slightly more than half.Home ownership: "Over half of U.S. households own a home outright or have a fixed-rate mortgage," Gerlach pointed out. They therefore "haven't felt much impact" from higher interest rates over the past couple of years. The other 42% have faced either higher mortgage payments on their adjustable-rate mortgages or higher rents, which in turn have delayed or dashed their hopes of buying a home. People in these circumstances "likely rate economic conditions as poor."Retail sales: "The traveling class has money," Gerlach noted, as evidenced by restaurants that are "packed" and "demand for travel [that] is robust." On the other hand, "retailers catering to budget-conscious consumers are struggling with inflation." Employment: Depending on the government report, employment over the last year has grown only modestly or by a huge amount. The government's "household" survey, which is used to determine the unemployment rate, reports that "376,000 more people are working" than a year ago, according to Gerlach's calculations. In contrast, according to the job-growth report that the government also publishes, which is based on surveys of businesses, 2.4 million jobs have been created over the last year. "If the household survey better reflects economic reality, it may help explain why 68% of respondents consider economic conditions only fair or poor."

The bottom line? Avoid the tendency to veer off toward one side or the other in these polarities. Investment success requires recognizing that both contain a piece of the truth.

The numbers: Home prices in the 20 biggest U.S. metros climbed to a new record high in May, but showed signs of losing steam as interest rates remain high.

The S&P CoreLogic Case-Shiller 20-city house-price index rose 0.3% in May compared to the previous month. 

Home prices in the 20 major U.S. metro markets were up 6.8% in the last 12 months ending in May, matching expectations from economists surveyed by Dow Jones Newswires and The Wall Street Journal.

That’s a deceleration compared to an increase of 7.3% the previous month.

A broader measure of home prices, the national index, rose 0.3% in May and was also up 5.9% over the past year. All numbers are seasonally adjusted. 

The 20-city and the national index are at an all-time high. 

Key details: New York posted the biggest year-over-year home-price gains in May. Prices were up 9.4%. 

All 20 major markets reported yearly gains for the sixth month in a row, S&P said.

Home prices grew the slowest in Portland, by 1%.

A separate report from the Federal Housing Finance Agency also showed home prices were flat in May compared with the previous month, and were up 5.7% in the past year. “The slowdown in U.S. house price appreciation continued in May amid a slight rise in both mortgage rates and housing inventory,” the agency said.

The median price of a resale home was $417,200 in May, and a newly built home was $407,100.

Big picture: Though the pace of home-price appreciation slowed in May, they still climbed to another all-time high.

High mortgage rates are weighing on home buyers, yet the market is still seeing demand for homes outpace the supply of home listings, which is pushing prices up. 

Some home buyers are opting to wait until the Federal Reserve cuts rates before buying a property. Others are weighing major upcoming changes regarding how real-estate agents are paid, which could also likely impact how much they can afford to buy. 

What S&P said: “All 20 markets observed annual gains for the last six months. The last time we saw that long a streak … [was] during the COVID housing boom,” Brian D. Luke, head of commodities, real estate and digital assets at S&P Dow Jones Indices, said in a statement.

“This rally pales in comparison in both duration and annual gains,” he added, but the bottom line is that buyers playing “the waiting game for the possibility of favorable changes in lending rates” might find it “costly … as home prices march forward.”

What are they saying? “The latest S&P CoreLogic Case-Shiller Index showed home sales prices continued to climb higher through May but the rate of growth is decelerating,” Ralph McLaughlin, senior economist at Realtor.com, said in a statement.

“We should expect the rate of home price growth to continue to slow over the next several months as the effects of high spring mortgage rates manifest themselves into prices,” he added. “After that, the trajectory of price growth is going to largely be determined by how deep and frequent the Fed cuts rates as well as how desperate sellers will become.”

Its dual mandate is to promote "maximum employment and stable prices in the U.S. economy." Stable prices mean the Fed tries to keep inflation in check, with its long-term annual target at 2%. 

To control inflation, one of the Fed's main tools is the federal funds rate, which is the rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. If that rate rises, banks generally pass on their additional cost.

Even though the Fed does not directly control all interest rates in the country, when it raises the fed funds rate, other interest rates eventually follow, including adjustable-rate mortgages, credit cards, home equity lines of credit, and other loans.

What is inflation? 
Inflation is a generalized rise in prices, affecting different goods and services throughout the economy, such as gas, rent and food. 

It can be caused by several factors, such as more people spending money on goods or services that are not readily available to meet that demand. That allows producers and service providers to raise prices without worrying about a significant loss in sales.

Inflation also could be caused by a shortage of supply. If there are not enough goods to meet the demand for a good or service, this could lead to an increase in a manufacturer's or retailer's wholesale costs, which, in turn, would be passed along to consumers through higher retail prices.
