ESG management has become business critical
Once purely a financial services term, ESG is now routinely used in board rooms as the useful collective descriptor for the range of aspects that relate to how a business interacts with society and the environment.

It spans climate change and wider sustainability, environmental compliance and liability management, health, safety and wellbeing, diversity and inclusion, employee development and retention, customer welfare, community relations, and business ethics and governance, amongst others.

Crucially, it is now understood that as both risks and opportunities, ESG factors are central to long-term commercial success. Environmental Social Governance management has become business critical.

Yet, whilst ESG is now in common business use, investment and the accessing of capital often still drives and defines the form of actions taken. From pension fund managers seeking to win new mandates, through to asset managers helping to invest that capital, to public or private businesses providing the opportunities for investment – actions are required to demonstrate that ESG risks and opportunities are being managed.

What does ESG mean?

ESG refers to a range of issues that fall under the umbrella terms of Environmental, Social, and Governance, used to measure how a business interacts with society and the environment. As potentially both risks and opportunities, and increasingly recognised as central to the long-term commercial success of a business, many investors are increasingly scrutinising and prioritising these factors as part of their financial analysis process.

**Mike:** what businesses are good prospects for ESG?

**GPT:** Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) principles are applicable across all industries and sectors. However, some types of businesses may have more direct and immediate opportunities to benefit from ESG considerations. Here are a few:

1. **Energy Companies**: Both renewable and non-renewable energy companies can benefit from ESG, as they directly impact the environment. Renewable energy companies often align well with ESG principles, while non-renewable energy companies can use ESG to identify opportunities to reduce their environmental impact and increase sustainability.

2. **Manufacturing Firms**: These businesses often have a significant environmental footprint and may face various social and governance challenges. ESG can help them identify ways to reduce their environmental impact, improve their labor practices, and enhance their governance structures.

3. **Real Estate and Construction**: These sectors can benefit from ESG through green building practices and ensuring fair labor practices.

4. **Banks and Financial Institutions**: These businesses can integrate ESG into their investment decision-making processes. They can also provide ESG-themed financial products or loans.

5. **Technology Companies**: They can benefit from ESG by addressing issues like e-waste, energy consumption, data privacy, and security.

6. **Agriculture and Food Production**: These companies can use ESG to address issues related to sustainable farming practices, fair labor practices, and food safety.

7. **Retail Companies**: They can benefit from ESG by addressing issues related to supply chain management, fair labor practices, and product sustainability.

In essence, any business that wishes to improve its long-term sustainability, risk management, and stakeholder relationships can be a good prospect for ESG.
