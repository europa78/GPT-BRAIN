### 1. **CPI and Inflation**

### 2. **Impact of CPI on Federal Reserve Decisions**
   - The Federal Reserve (Fed) closely monitors inflation data, including CPI, to determine its monetary policy. The Fed’s dual mandate is to ensure price stability (control inflation) and maximize employment.
   - If inflation is **high or rising**, the Fed may raise interest rates to cool the economy and prevent inflation from getting out of control. Higher rates make borrowing more expensive, reducing spending and investment, which can slow down inflation.
   - Conversely, if inflation is **low or falling**, the Fed may lower interest rates to stimulate the economy by making borrowing cheaper, encouraging spending and investment, and thereby supporting economic growth.

### 3. **Current Context: Lower-than-Expected CPI**

   - **Larger Rate Cut:** A lower CPI would give the Fed more room to cut interest rates aggressively. A larger rate cut, such as 50 basis points, would be intended to stimulate the economy by making borrowing cheaper, encouraging investment and spending.

### 4. **Higher Inflation and Its Implications**
   - **Higher-than-expected CPI** suggests that inflation is persistent or even accelerating. This would signal that inflationary pressures remain strong, potentially putting upward pressure on prices.

### 5. **Market Expectations and Reactions**
   - Investors and traders closely watch CPI data because it influences their expectations of Fed policy. A milder inflation reading released Wednesday removes one of the last hurdles the Federal Reserve needed to clear before cutting rates in September.

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) increased 2.9% over the prior year in July, down from June's 3% annual gain in prices. On a "core" basis, which strips out the more volatile costs of food and gas, prices in July climbed 3.2% over last year — down from 3.3% in June. That was the smallest increase since April 2021.

"I think this report is a green light for the Federal Reserve in September," Nathan Sheets, global chief economist for Citigroup, told Yahoo Finance.

The new numbers are the latest confirmation that inflation is in fact cooling once again after heating back up during the first quarter of the year, a development that prompted the Fed to warn at one point that rates would likely stay higher for longer.

Fed Chair Jerome Powell made it clear at the end of last month that a cut in September was “on the table” as long as the data supported it. He and other policymakers have said they want to be sure that inflation is in fact moving “sustainably” down to their 2% goal.

"It’s just a question of seeing more good data," Powell said at a press conference on July 31. "We just want to see more and gain confidence."

"I don't think there's really any debate that the Fed is cutting in September," JPMorgan Chase's (JPM) Kelsey Berro told Yahoo Finance earlier this week.

"What we're really debating is [whether or not it's] going to be [a] 25 or ... 50 [basis point rate cut]," added Berro, who is an asset management portfolio manager for global fixed income.
