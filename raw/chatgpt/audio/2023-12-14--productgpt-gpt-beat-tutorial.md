**GPT:** When you sell a beat pack on Gumroad, the transfer of rights to the purchaser depends on the licensing agreement that you provide with the beat pack. There are typically two types of rights that can be sold with music beats: non-exclusive rights and exclusive rights.

1. **Non-Exclusive Rights**: This type of license allows the purchaser to use the beat, but the producer (you) retains the right to sell the same beat to other buyers. Non-exclusive licenses usually come with certain restrictions, such as limits on the number of streams or sales the purchaser can have with the track they create using the beat.

2. **Exclusive Rights**: Selling exclusive rights means that once the beat is purchased, you can no longer sell that beat to anyone else. The purchaser has the exclusive right to use the beat for their music projects. This typically involves a higher price due to the rights being transferred and the exclusivity of the purchase.

For either type of sale, it's crucial to have a clear licensing agreement that outlines:

- The type of rights being sold (non-exclusive or exclusive).
- Any restrictions on the use of the beat.
- Whether or not the purchaser can monetize the music they make with the beat.
- Credits and royalties arrangements.

It's important to specify in your Gumroad product description and terms of sale what type of rights are included with the purchase. If you're selling exclusive rights, you should also include a contract that both you and the buyer will sign to formalize the transfer of these rights. This contract should detail all the terms of use, including the extent of the rights transferred, any limitations, and the obligations of both parties.

If you do not specify that the beats come with exclusive rights, the default assumption is usually that the rights are non-exclusive, and you retain ownership and the right to sell the beats to other artists. Always consult with a legal professional to ensure your sales terms and licensing agreements are clear and enforceable.

5. **Performance License**: This is necessary for music that will be played publicly, whether live, on the radio, in a business, or streamed online. Performance Rights Organizations (PROs) often manage these licenses.

6. **Print Rights License**: This is used for the distribution of printed music, like sheet music or lyrics. It allows the buyer to reproduce the written form of the song.

7. **Master License**: Often paired with a sync license, this gives the buyer the right to use a pre-recorded version of your song in a visual or audio project. This is different from a sync license, which does not inherently include the rights to a specific recording of the song.

8. **Blanket License**: This is often used by broadcasters or venues. It allows them to use any music in a particular catalog as much as they want over a specified period.

Each type of license serves different purposes and offers various levels of exclusivity and usage rights. When choosing the type of license to sell your music under, consider factors like your control over the music, the potential revenue, and the intended use by the buyer. 6. **Royalties**: Depending on the agreement, the producer may still be entitled to royalties from the use of the music, especially if it’s used in a revenue-generating capacity.

7. **Credit**: The license may require the user to credit the producer of the music in their project.
