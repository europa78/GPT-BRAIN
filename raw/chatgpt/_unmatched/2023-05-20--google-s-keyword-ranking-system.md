# Google's Keyword Ranking System

## Transcript

**Mike:** Break down the hierarchy of keywords Google uses as the basis of their ranking system

**GPT:** Google's search engine uses a complex algorithm to rank web pages based on various factors, including the use of keywords. However, it's important to clarify that Google doesn't use a "hierarchy" of keywords in a traditional sense. Instead, it uses a variety of factors to determine the relevance and quality of a page for a specific search query. As of my knowledge cutoff in September 2021, here are some of the ways Google's search engine algorithm considers keywords:

1. **Keyword relevance**: Google looks for keywords in important places on your webpage, such as the title tag, meta description, header tags (H1, H2, etc.), and throughout the body content. The algorithm uses these keywords to understand the topic of your content and rank it for relevant searches.

2. **Keyword density**: This is the ratio of a keyword or phrase to the total number of words on a page. Google uses this to determine if a page is relevant to a specific keyword. However, keyword density isn't as important as it used to be, and keyword stuffing (overuse of a keyword) can lead to penalties.

3. **Long-tail keywords**: These are more specific keyword phrases that visitors are more likely to use when they're closer to the point of purchase or when they're using voice search. They are generally easier to rank for due to less competition.

4. **LSI (Latent Semantic Indexing) keywords**: These are keywords semantically related to your primary keyword. They help Google understand the context of your content.

5. **Search intent**: Google tries to understand the intent behind a user's search, and it will consider keywords that match this intent. For example, if a user is likely looking for information ("what is X"), Google will favor informational content. If a user is looking to buy something ("buy X"), Google will favor ecommerce pages.

It's also important to note that Google's algorithm considers many other factors in addition to keywords, such as backlinks (links from other websites to your page), site speed, mobile-friendliness, and many others. The exact workings of Google's algorithm are not publicly disclosed and are frequently updated, so the specifics can change over time.

**Mike:** Give me an example of number one or two

**GPT:** Sure, I can provide an example for each of the two points:

1. **Keyword relevance**: Let's say you have a website that sells handmade leather shoes, and you want your website to rank for the keyword "handmade leather shoes". Here's how you might optimize for keyword relevance:

   - **Title tag**: "Handmade Leather Shoes - Your Store Name"
   - **Meta description**: "Discover high-quality, durable, and stylish handmade leather shoes at Your Store Name. Explore our collection today."
   - **Header tags**: You might have a H1 tag on your page that says "Handmade Leather Shoes", and H2 tags for subheadings like "Why Choose Our Handmade Leather Shoes" or "The Process Behind Our Handmade Leather Shoes".
   - **Body content**: You'd also naturally incorporate the keyword throughout your webpage content, explaining the materials, craftsmanship, and benefits of your handmade leather shoes.

2. **Keyword density**: The keyword density is the ratio of the number of times the keyword appears in the content to the total number of words in the content. For example, if your webpage has 1000 words, and you use your keyword "handmade leather shoes" 10 times, then your keyword density for "handmade leather shoes" would be 1% (10 / 1000 * 100).

   It's crucial, however, not to overdo it with keyword density. If the same webpage used the keyword "handmade leather shoes" 100 times, then the keyword density would be 10%—which could be seen as keyword stuffing, a practice that Google's algorithm tends to penalize because it can disrupt the natural flow of text and negatively impact user experience. It's generally advised to aim for a keyword density of around 1-2%.

**Mike:** List white hat SEO methods

**GPT:** White hat SEO refers to the use of optimization strategies and techniques that focus on a human audience as opposed to search engines and completely follows search engine rules and policies. Here are some common white hat SEO methods:

1. **Keyword Research and Use**: This involves finding the right keywords that your target audience uses to find the products or services you offer. Once you have the keywords, you integrate them naturally into your content. 

2. **Content Creation**: Quality content is crucial in white hat SEO. This means creating unique, relevant, and valuable content that provides information to the reader, not just content stuffed with keywords. 

3. **Meta Tags Optimization**: This involves optimizing title tags, meta descriptions, header tags, and alt tags with relevant keywords and useful descriptions.

4. **Site Architecture Optimization**: This means structuring your website in a way that is easy to navigate for users and search engine bots. This includes using a clean URL structure, creating an XML sitemap, and using breadcrumb navigation.

5. **Mobile Optimization**: As more users search on mobile devices, having a mobile-optimized website is crucial. This includes ensuring your site is responsive and loads quickly on mobile devices.

6. **Page Speed Optimization**: Google considers page speed as one of the ranking factors. Therefore, it's important to ensure that your website loads quickly.

7. **Use of Schema Markup**: This helps search engines understand your website better, potentially improving how your site is represented in search results.

8. **Backlinking**: This involves earning backlinks from reputable websites. White hat methods include producing high-quality content that others want to link to, guest blogging, and building relationships with influencers in your industry.

9. **Local SEO**: If your business serves a specific area, optimizing for local search is essential. This includes creating a Google My Business listing and getting listed in local directories.

10. **User Experience Optimization**: Google's algorithm considers user experience factors, like how quickly users leave your site (bounce rate), how many pages they visit, and how long they stay.

Remember, white hat SEO strategies are about creating a great website with valuable content and a good user experience, while also making it easy for search engines to understand and index your site. It takes time, but the results are more sustainable in the long run compared to black hat techniques, which can lead to penalties from search engines.

**Mike:** List black hat SEO methods

**GPT:** Black hat SEO refers to practices that violate search engine guidelines in an attempt to boost a page's ranking in search results. These tactics are often manipulative and short-term, and they can lead to search engines penalizing or even banning your site. Here are some common black hat SEO methods:

1. **Keyword Stuffing**: This involves overloading a webpage's content or meta tags with keywords in an attempt to manipulate a site's ranking in Google search results.

2. **Hidden Text and Links**: Some websites hide text or links within their content to manipulate search engine rankings. This could be done by making the font color the same as the background color, setting the font size to zero, or placing a link on a specific character such as a hyphen.

3. **Cloaking**: This is a practice where the content presented to the search engine spider is different from that presented to the user's browser, essentially showing different versions of a webpage to search engines and users.

4. **Doorway Pages**: These are pages that are optimized for a specific keyword and are designed to capture the attention of search engines but then redirect visitors to a different page.

5. **Article Spinning**: This is when a site takes an original piece of content, like a blog post, and rewords it with synonyms and phrases to create "new" content. This is done to trick search engines into thinking the content is unique and fresh.

6. **Link Farms, Link Wheels or Link Networks**: These involve creating networks of pages that link to each other in an attempt to increase backlinks and improve the ranking of a certain page. 

7. **Negative SEO**: This involves sabotaging a competitor’s rankings in search results. This could be done by hacking a website or through techniques such as link spamming.

8. **Duplicate Content**: This is when large blocks of content across or within domains are either completely match other content or are appreciably similar.

9. **Rich Snippet Markup Spam**: This involves manipulating structured data or schema markup to mislead search engines and users.

10. **Automated Content**: This is content generated programmatically (like using AI or bots) rather than written by humans, often resulting in poor quality and nonsensical text.

These practices are not recommended. Not only can they lead to penalties, but they also create a poor user experience, which can harm your site's reputation and performance in the long term. It's better to focus on white hat SEO techniques, which comply with search engine guidelines and focus on providing value to users.
